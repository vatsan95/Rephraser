Free Grammarly Alternative That Works Offline (Mac + Windows)

Published April 19, 2026 · 7 min read · by Srivatsan Raghavan

If you're searching for a free alternative to Grammarly that doesn't send your text to a cloud server, this post is the short answer: Rephraser. It's free, open-source (MIT), runs entirely on your laptop, and works on both Mac and Windows. The long answer, and why this matters, is below.

The problem with mainstream AI writing tools

Grammarly, Microsoft Copilot, Wordtune, ChatGPT, and Claude all share the same basic architecture: you type, the text is sent over the internet to a server, a model runs there, and the result comes back. For casual rewriting, that's fine. For anything sensitive, it's a problem.

"Sensitive" covers a larger surface than most people realise: contract language, legal drafts, medical notes, client-confidential communication, internal company strategy, personal messages to family, NDA-covered work, anything a lawyer would say "don't share this with a third party." A cloud AI tool is a third party. Its terms of service may or may not allow your text to be used for training. You don't always get to choose.

Even Apple Intelligence, which advertises on-device processing, falls back to "Private Cloud Compute" or ChatGPT for requests that the on-device model can't handle. That fallback is explicit in Apple's own documentation. It's a useful feature, but it means the guarantee isn't absolute.

What Rephraser does differently

Rephraser runs an AI model locally on your machine. You download the model once (between 400 MB and 2.5 GB depending on which you pick — Gemma 3, Qwen 2.5, Phi-4 Mini, or Llama 3.2), and after that, every rephrase runs on your CPU or GPU. No network calls. You can use it with Wi-Fi off.

The workflow is deliberately minimal: select text anywhere in any app, press ⌥⇧R on Mac or Ctrl+Alt+R on Windows, a small panel appears with the rewrite streaming in, and Enter pastes the result. It takes about 1.4 seconds on an M2 MacBook Air with the default Gemma 3 1B model.

The claim: "Rephraser runs entirely on-device" isn't a marketing slogan — it's auditable. The full source is MIT licensed on GitHub. There is exactly one HTTP client in the Windows code (models.rs, used only to download GGUF model files from HuggingFace on your command). There is no code path that sends text anywhere.

Who Rephraser is for

  • Lawyers and paralegals who need to rewrite contract clauses without shipping them to third-party servers.
  • Medical professionals drafting notes or correspondence that must stay HIPAA-adjacent.
  • Privacy-conscious writers who want AI assistance without the telemetry.
  • Developers and technical writers working on codebases or docs under NDA.
  • Anyone who lives on flaky internet — on a plane, on a train, at a cafe with 3G — and needs rewriting to just work.
  • People who are tired of $12/mo subscriptions for things that should be local software.

Who it's not for

  • If you need grammar correction specifically (not tone rewriting), Grammarly is better and will stay better. Rephraser rewrites; it doesn't proofread.
  • If you need translation, use DeepL or Google Translate — not a rephraser.
  • If you write professionally in a language other than English and want nuanced style advice, the current models are weaker on non-English tasks.
  • If you need team features, cloud sync, or a web version, Rephraser won't help. It's a local tool by design.

What about Apple Intelligence on Mac?

Apple Intelligence is a solid choice on Mac if you have an M-series chip and are running macOS 15+. It's free, well-integrated, and most processing stays on-device. Two reasons you might still prefer Rephraser:

  1. Apple Intelligence offloads to cloud for requests its small on-device model can't handle. Rephraser never does.
  2. Apple Intelligence is Apple-only. Rephraser gives you the same tool on your Windows work laptop.

What about Microsoft Copilot on Windows?

Copilot is integrated into Windows and works well — but every request goes to Microsoft's servers (and through OpenAI). If your employer or your personal standards require local-only processing, Copilot is off the table. Rephraser fills that gap.

FAQ

Is Rephraser really free forever?

Yes. MIT license. No "premium" tier planned. If you want to support development, there's a PayPal link in the footer, but it's always optional.

Does Rephraser use my text to train a model?

No. There's no network call that sends your text anywhere. The AI model runs on your laptop and has no feedback loop to any server.

How big is the install?

The app itself is ~8 MB (Mac) or ~15 MB (Windows). The AI model is a separate 400 MB to 2.5 GB one-time download, which you pick at first launch. Smaller models are faster; larger models produce better rewrites.

Will it slow down my laptop?

Idle usage is ~150 MB RAM. While a rephrase is running, it can spike to 1.5 GB RAM (Gemma 3 1B) or 3 GB+ (Gemma 3 4B). CPU usage is near 100% during the 1–3 second rephrase, then drops back to zero. If you have at least 4 GB free RAM and any x64 CPU from the last 8 years, you're fine.

What's the catch?

Honest answer: the Windows build isn't code-signed yet, so Windows SmartScreen shows an "Unknown publisher" warning on first install. You click More info → Run anyway and it's whitelisted. A code-signing certificate is on the to-do list after 100+ installs.
